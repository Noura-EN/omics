02 / Overview About the workshop

Background.

The development of next-generation sequencing and single-cell technologies brought data analysis to the forefront of molecular biology research. In 2026, with fast-paced advances in AI, our power to derive insight from this data is only increasing.

Agentic tools can now act as co-scientists — planning and executing multi-step bioinformatics analyses across the breadth of available tools, synthesising across transcriptomics, single-cell, and multi-omics workflows. They also bring their own challenges: long-context handling, reproducibility, data privacy, the absence of ground-truth, vast hypothesis spaces, and incomplete data.

At the cusp of this shift — when benchmarks are not yet established and know-how is unevenly distributed and siloed — this workshop brings the omics community together to set common ground.

Confused or misinformed use of agentic AI, even with the best intentions, will hinder omics efforts. Equally, if legacy pipelines persist, the significant value-add of AI will be missed entirely. — Workshop premise
03 / Scope Three core areas

What we will address.

Capabilities & limitations of AI agents

  • What AI innovations in other domains have yet to be applied to omics?
  • How do agents expand the space of feasible analysis?

Co-scientist: integration into workflows

  • How can human-in-the-loop workflows make agent–human collaboration more than the sum of its parts?
  • What risks do AI agents bring to current workflows, and how can these be overcome?
  • What is bottlenecking adoption — in academia and industry?

Benchmarking, evaluation & reporting

  • How should we evaluate end-to-end agent performance on scientific workflows?
  • What metrics capture scientific correctness, not just code correctness?
  • What constitutes a reproducible, auditable agent-driven analysis?

06 / Submit Call for papers

Submissions.

What we’re looking for

  • Capabilities & limitations Novel agent architectures, tool-use strategies, or capability evaluations relevant to omics.
  • Workflow integration Human-in-the-loop systems, adoption case studies, risk assessments.
  • Benchmarks & evaluation New benchmarks, metrics for scientific correctness, reproducibility frameworks.
  • Position pieces Reasoned arguments on standards, governance, or community norms.

Logistics

  • Format Short papers (≤ 5 pages) and long papers — we accept both to widen access for early-stage researchers.
  • Anonymisation Submissions must be anonymised. Non-anonymised papers will be desk-rejected.
  • Conflict of interest No organiser reviews a submission from their own organisation.
  • Dual submission Work presented at the NeurIPS main conference cannot also appear here. Authors confirm compliance at submission.
  • Submission portal OpenReview link to follow.
